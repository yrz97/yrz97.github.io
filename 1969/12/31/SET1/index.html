The SET-binding and cohesin-binding domains in Sgo1 are in close proximity, but separable

  1. try to find the region responsible for SET binding $\rightarrow$ using different length of GST-tagged Sgo1 N-terminal fragments to pull down His6-tagged SET $\rightarrow$ the region 280–310 is the major domain required for SET binding:Fig 1B
  2. ==??== find the region they determined is different from other studies(residue 1-40), it might be the application of different assays.
  3. find the SET binding domain is close to cohesion binding domain: Fig 1 C$\rightarrow$ determine if the truncation of SET domain affect the Sgo1-cohesion binding,and vice versa:

in virtro: Fig 1 D

  • using GST-Sgo1(include these two regions) to pull down His6-SET or cohesin: find can bind both
  • using GSTSgo1 ΔSET to pull down His6-SET or cohesin: find it can bind cohesion,not SET
  • using GSTSgo1 Δcohesion to pull down His6-SET or cohesin: find it can bind SET,not cohesion

in cells:
found that Myc-Sgo1 WT, ΔSET, ΔN, and ΔSET ΔN all bound Smc1, a cohesin subunit, and PP2A at comparable levels: Fig 1G

conclusion: these two domains can be separable from each other despite their proximity

  1. evaluate the extent ==2== contribute to Sgo1-SET binding:
  • Myc-Sgo1 WT robustly bound GFP-SET, whereas Myc-Sgo1 ΔSET
    and ΔSET ΔN completely lost their binding to GFP-SET
  • using Myc-Sgo1 ΔN (residues 1–40) and Myc-Sgo1 can ip GFP-SET successfully and extent similiar: Fig.1F

conclusion: 280–310 region in Sgo1 is the major binding site for SET, and the 1–40 region of Sgo1 is dispensable for SET binding in cells.

[SET ΔC]: it’s the C terminal of SET, which is the PP2A binding domain and it’s depletion will also decrease Sgo1 binding.

SET can disrupt the Sgo1–cohesin interaction in vitro and in cells

  1. Speculated: that SET and cohesin could compete with one another for binding Sgo1, because the SET and cohesin-binding domains in Sgo1 are in close proximity; Besides, because Cdk1 phosphorylation of Sgo1 at Thr346 significantly enhances the Sgo1–cohesin binding,inclusion of phospho-Sgo1 in this experiment would enable us to evaluate in more detail how SET competes with cohesin for Sgo1 binding
  • in virtro:
    using GST-Sgo1(phospho-Sgo1 or nonphospho-Sgo1) to pull down His6-SET or SA2/Scc $\rightarrow$SET disrupted the Sgo1–cohesin binding in a dose-dependent manner regardless of the phosphorylation state of Sgo1 at T346 :Fig 3A

?: failed to observe a significant difference in resisting SET competition between mock teated and Cdk1-treated Sgo1

answer: Because we could not quantitatively measure how much Sgo1 was phosphorylated by Cdk1 in this in vitro experiment, the above result might be due to an outcome of low efficiency in Cdk1 phosphorylation.

  • in cell:

  1. using Sgo1 to ip coheison with or without GFP-SET$\rightarrow$ GFP-SET overexpression moderately reduced the Sgo1–cohesin binding by more than twofold: Fig 3B and C.

  2. to further confirm if the disruption of the Sgo1–cohesin interaction is mediated by SET binding to Sgo1$\rightarrow$ using Myc-Sgo1 to ip Smc1 with overexpressing GFP-SET WT and 3K: Fig. 3, D and E

overexpressing GFP-SET WT overexpressing GFP-SET WT
Myc-Sgo1 bound more GFP-SET but less Smc1 barely affected the Myc-Sgo1-Smc1 binding but decreased its binding with Myc-Sgo1

  1. expressing GFP-SET at distinct levels and found that SET disrupted
    the Sgo1–cohesin interaction in a dose-dependent manner(ps:this experiment can correspond to in virtro experiment: Fig 3A):Fig 3F

  2. as Cdk1-phosphorylated Sgo1 at Thr346 is essential for the Sgo1–cohesin interaction $\rightarrow$ examined Sgo1 phospho-Thr346 levels in cells overexpressing SET $\rightarrow$ found that Sgo1 phospho-Thr346 levels were comparable in mock cells and cells expressing GFP-SET WT or 3K: Fig. 3, D and G $\rightarrow$ suggesting that the difference in the Sgo1–cohesin binding observed above is unlikely due to changes in phospho-Thr346 levels( understanding: because p-Sgo1 is unchanged, so it’s not possible that SET$\uparrow$ $\rightarrow$ p-Sgo1 $\downarrow$ $\rightarrow$ Sgo1-cohesion $\downarrow$)．

  3. Besides,no significant difference was detected in Sgo1–PP2A interactions in GFP-SET WT and 3K cells, suggesting: Fig 3D

conclusion: SET binding to Sgo1 can, at least moderately, inhibit the Sgo1–cohesin interaction

Chromosome segregation is delayed in cells expressing Sgo1 ΔSET or depleted of SET

[delayed]: it means it experiences longer time from metaphase to anaphase

  1. It has been shown that delayed cohesion resolution can postpone chromosome segregation$\rightarrow$ as SET binding to Sgo1 might inhibit the Sgo1–cohesin interaction, so monitored chromosome segregation in Mock,si-separase and si-SET $\rightarrow$ these three spent duration of 17 min from NEB to metaphase, but as for the duration from metaphase to chromosome segeration, they experience 14min,30 min and 23.1 min respectively:Fig. 4, A and B; and Fig. S3 A

conclusion: SET functions to promote timely chromosome segregation during mitosis

  1. to further explore the importance of the SET-Sgo1 binding in chromosome segregation, we examined mitotic progression in Sgo1 ΔSET cells
  • cells stably expressing Sgo1 WT or ΔSET were transfected with plasmids after Sgo1 depletion

results:

  1. Expression of Sgo1 WT or ΔSET significantly rescued the prometaphase arrest caused by si-Sgo1 in ∼80% of cells: Fig 1I
  2. The cells expressing with Sgo1 WT or ΔSET progressed from NEB to metaphase exhibited no significant difference compared with Mock cells: Fig S3 B,but the cell expressing Sgo1 ΔSET were delayed in chromosome segregation compared with the cells expressing Sgo1 WT: Fig 4C and D.

conclusion: SET and the SET-Sgo1 binding may promote chromosome segregation via disrupting the Sgo1–cohesin interaction.

[overexpressing]:As exogenous SET was always expressed at much higher levels than endogenous SET, we were only able to assess the role of SET 3K in chromosome segregation under the condition of its overexpression
[was slightly delayed]: this delay is caused by separated sister chromatid in advance, so that check mechanism arrest them

however: we cannot completely rule out the possibility that the delayed chromosome segregation might be caused by other mitotic defects induced by SET depletion or expression of Sgo1 ΔSET, such as subtle kinetochore-microtubule attachment defects

  1. further attempted to confirm the importance of the SET-Sgo1 binding in chromosome segregation using the SET 3K$\rightarrow$chromosome segregation was slightly delayed in overexpressing SET WT compared with cells overexpressing the SET 3K:Fig. S3 C, and Moreover, a higher incidence of chromosome segregation defects was also observed in cells overexpressing GFP-SET WT than 3K: Fig. S3 D.

conclusion: these results further support that the SET–Sgo1 binding regulates chromosome segregation

?? : the delayed anaphase in overexpresing SET-WT is very likely different from that in Sgo1 ΔSET,the slight delay for SET-WT is likely derived from weakened centromeric cohesion caused by its overexpression: Fig. 5, C–E, but the delay for Sgo1 ΔSET is due to a prolonged metaphase-to-anaphase transition: Fig 4C, D.
[MG132-treated]:can induce centromeric cohesion fatigue,but for nocodazole-treated,cohesion fatigue is inhibited by drug-induced depolymerization of mitotic spindle microtubule,so can’t see the phenomena,so MG132 could be used to detect mild cohesion defects by measuring kinetics of cohesion fatigue.
[cohesion fatigue]:this means that spindle pulling forces can cause asynchronous chromatid separation,that is some sister chromatid seperation happened too early.

SET overexpression accelerates the occurrence of cohesion fatigue in MG132-arrested cells, not in nocodazole-arrested cells

  1. test if SET overexpression could impair centromeric cohesion in cells

in RPE1 cell:

  • nocodazale-treated:

  1. demonstrated exogenous SET is overexpressed: expression levels of GFP-SET WT and 3K proteins were comparable to one another, and both of them were higher than endogenous SET proteins: Fig 5A

  2. using ip, found SET overexpression in nocodazole-arrested prometaphase cells did not induce any discernible increase in centromeric cohesion defects compared with mock treatment: Fig 5B

suggest: either the amount of overexpressed SET proteins in our experimental condition does not suffice to completely disrupt the Sgo1–cohesin interaction or overexpressed SET proteins are functionally suppressed in nocodazole-arrested cells

but: we have shown that SET-GFP overexpression in nocodazole-arrested cells was able to moderately reduce but not abolish the Sgo1–cohesin interaction:Fig. 3, B, D, and F

  • MG132-treated:

1.MG132 treatment for 4 h only induced cohesion fatigue in 5% of mock cells,whereas it did so in 28% cells overexpressing GFP-SET WT,besides, GFP-SET 3K overexpression did also induce a slight
increase in cells with cohesion fatigue (10% versus 5%), the
extent was much milder than WT overexpression. $\rightarrow$ SET overexpression can accelerate the occurrence of cohesion fatigue: Fig. 5, C and D

in Hela cell: similar results: Fig. 5, E and F, and Fig. S4 A

  1. Furthermore, examined the capacity of GFP-SET ΔC and 3K-ΔC in inducing cohesion fatigue in MG132-arrested HeLa Tet-On cells $\rightarrow$ failed to induce cohesion fatigue

but: as truncation of the SET C-terminus decreased its binding not only to PP2A but also to Sgo1: Fig. 2 E and Fig. S2 C , the above results might not be sufficient to support that SET directly inhibiting PP2A activity is also involved in this process.

conclusion: As the only detected defect of the SET 3K mutant is in Sgo1 binding, the SET overexpression–induced centromeric cohesion defects, we believe, are likely through disruption of the Sgo1–cohesin interaction.

  1. SET was recently shown to be able to evict Sgo1 from chromatin in vitro system $\rightarrow$ quantified the total Sgo1 levels at kinetochores and inner centromeres in HeLa Tet-On cells overexpressing GFP-SET, instead, a slight increase was observed in prometaphase/metaphase cells :Fig. S4, B and C

conclusion: the centromeric cohesion defects caused by SET overexpression in our experimental system are unlikely through prematurely removing Sgo1 away from chromatin.

####SET depletion delays the occurrence of cohesion fatigue in MG132-arrested cells and reduces Sgo1 relocation from inner centromeres to kinetochores

  1. tested if reducing SET protein levels could delay occurrence of cohesion fatigue
  • Western blots demonstrated that SET siRNAs efficiently depleted SET proteins:Fig 6A
  • after MG132 treatment for 2 h and 4 h triggered the occurrence of
    cohesion fatigue in mock cells, siSET cells exhibited less cohesion fatigue: Fig 6B $\rightarrow$ t SET depletion delays the occurrence of cohesion fatigue
  1. The delayed occurrence of cohesion fatigue might also be caused by increased Sgo1 proteins at kinetochores and inner centromeres induced by SET depletion $\rightarrow$ quantified the total Sgo1 signals at kinetochores and inner centromeres in MG132-arrested mock and siSET cells $\rightarrow$ No significant increase in Sgo1 signals was observed in siSET cells compared with mock cells: Fig. S4 D
  2. transfected RNAi-resistant SET to these SET-depleted cells to
    test if it could restore cohesion fatigue $\rightarrow$ SET depletion significantly suppressed cohesion fatigue induced by MG132, and the suppression was completely reversed by expression of SET WT, but not SET 3K: Fig 6C

conclusin: Sgo1 overexpression is able to delay the occurrence of MG132-induced cohesion fatigue

4.Studies have shown that Sgo1 overexpression is able to delay the occurrence of MG132-induced cohesion fatigue $\rightarrow$ overexpression of Myc-Sgo1 WT or ΔSET both rescued MG132-induded cohesion fatigue, but no significant deference between them was observed, although ΔSET seemingly did so better than WT.

5.The Sgo1–cohesin interaction is required for Sgo1 localization to the inner centromere. If SET functions to inhibit the Sgo1–cohesin interaction, SET depletion would prevent the removal of Sgo1 from inner centromeres; on the contrary, SET overexpression would promote this process

  • examine Sgo1 localization only in chromosomes with intact centromeric cohesion could minimize this caveat, because in a portion of these chromosomes, Myc-Scc1 was still retained at inner centromeres even though Sgo1 had been relocated to kinetochores: Fig. S4 F
  • In the absence of MG132 treatment, Sgo1 predominantly localized at inner centromeres in the majority of the chromosomes of mock and siSET cells,At 2h and 4 h after MG132 treatment, Sgo1 localized at kinetochores in less cells in si-SET cells than mock cells:Fig 6 D,E
  • to further prove this,examined Sgo1 localization on chromosomes with intact centromeric cohesion in cells overexpressing SET $\rightarrow$ overexpression of SET WT triggered more Sgo1 localization to kinetochores than mock treatment,but the Sgo1 binding–deficient mutant SET 3K failed to do so:Fig 6F

conclusion:SET, by directly binding to Sgo1, may promote the removal of Sgo1 from inner centromeres. We prefer a direct role of SET in the removal of Sgo1 from inner centromeres rather than an
indirect consequence, although we cannot completely rule out
the latter.
